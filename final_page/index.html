Final Takeaway

Flooding is not just about rain.

Across these four flood events, the visualization shows how inland flooding becomes more dangerous when rainfall, runoff, geography, and a warmer atmosphere combine. Earlier floods were devastating because of river systems, terrain, and infrastructure, but later floods show how heat can intensify the water cycle by helping the atmosphere hold more moisture.

Heat loads the atmosphere

Warmer air can hold more moisture. This matters because a wetter atmosphere can release heavier rainfall during extreme events.

02

Rain becomes runoff

Precipitation alone does not explain the damage. The danger grows when rainfall turns into surface runoff that overwhelms rivers, slopes, and drainage systems.

03

Place changes the disaster

Flat river basins, steep mountains, and downstream provinces experience flooding differently. The same rainfall can create very different impacts depending on geography.

Why this visualization works

The maps let viewers compare flood events across time and place instead of reading them as isolated disasters. By moving from the Yellow River to the Yangtze, Vargas, and Pakistan, the project shows an escalating story: floods are shaped by local geography, but warming can increase the moisture and rainfall pressure behind them.

The one thing to remember: as the climate warms, inland flooding can become more intense because the atmosphere, land, and rivers are all pushed closer to their limits.
