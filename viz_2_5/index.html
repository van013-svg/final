Vargas Tragedy

1999 → Venezuela Coastal Mountains

The Vargas Tragedy was not a hurricane. It was a rainfall-driven disaster where extreme precipitation triggered flash floods, landslides, mudslides, and debris flows along Venezuela’s steep coastal mountains.

This flood is different from the Yellow River and Yangtze events because the danger came from intensity and terrain. When moisture-heavy air hit the mountains, rainfall concentrated quickly, and runoff rushed downhill instead of spreading slowly across a flat river basin.

In the project storyline, Vargas shows how warming and moisture can create not just bigger floods, but also faster and more violent inland runoff.
