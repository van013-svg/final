A historical baseline

The 1887 Yellow River Flood was one of China’s deadliest flood disasters. Heavy rainfall and a major dike failure allowed water to spread across the low-lying North China Plain, causing catastrophic inland flooding before modern industrial warming.

01 Historical Baseline

Yellow River Flood

1887 → North China Plain

The 1887 Yellow River Flood represents the project’s earlier baseline. This disaster happened before modern industrial warming became the dominant climate signal, so the flood was shaped more by heavy rain, dike failure, flat terrain, and river control problems.

The flood was still catastrophic because the Yellow River sits above parts of the surrounding plain from centuries of sediment buildup. Once the dikes failed, water spread quickly across low-lying farmland and settlements.

In this event, the temperature signal was not as intense as later floods, so the story is less about extreme heat and more about how rainfall and runoff became deadly when the basin had very little drainage room.

Key pattern

Earlier flooding was catastrophic even without modern heat extremes. Here, flat lowlands, heavy rain, and dike failure turned river overflow into widespread inland flooding.
